Task 7

The crisis in Syria is one of the most important reasons why ISIS grew capable of mounting such an effective attack on the Iraqi government. The chaos in Syria allowed ISIS to hold this territory pretty securely. This is a big deal in terms of weaponry and money. “The war gave them a lot of access to heavy weaponry”, Michael Knights said. ISIS also “has a funding stream available to them because of local businesses and the oil and gas sector”.

It's also hugely important as a safe zone. When fighting Syrian troops, ISIS can safely retreat to Iraq; when fighting Iraqis it can go to Syria. Statistical evidence says these safe “rear areas” help insurgents win: “one of the best predictors of insurgent success that we have to date is the presence of a rear area”, Jason Lyall said.

References: 11 facts that explain the escalating crisis in Iraq. Zack Beauchamp.


Task 8

There is no clear doctrine on the use of existing strategic reserves in Europe. There are many reasons, and many of them are quite convincing. For example, governments do not want to interfere in pricing, because, first, it is expensive, and secondly, the intervention is doomed to failure. There is little sense in the EU proposal on the establishment of a strategic reserve controlled by the European Commission to conduct countercyclical interventions, it has been sharply criticized by most experts - both practitioners and theorists, and must be dismissed. It has long been stipulated by economists, but in practice it did not find its solution. Therefore researchers state that it is best to treat strategic reserves as belonging to the state.


Task 9

The oil industry can no longer rely on its monopoly of the transport market. The vehicle industry is replacing oil with more efficient vehicles, and biofuels are replacing oil products as liquid fuels. This is driven both by the increase in oil prices since 2005, and by government policies limiting carbon emissions.

Since 2011 all major importing countries have adopted strong policies on carbon emissions and vehicle efficiency. These secure markets for efficient automobiles, rather than for oil. As current policies are unlikely to achieve their aims, it is probable that stronger policies will be introduced. The result will be to flatten and reverse growth in the use of petroleum in transport in developed countries, and slow its growth in developing countries.

References: What Next for the Oil and Gas Industry? John Mitchell, Valérie Marcel, Beth Mitchell.
